\chapter*{Conclusion and Perspectives}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Conclusion and Perspectives}

This report presented the work carried out during the end-of-study internship entitled
\textit{Knowledge Graph Exploration and Querying Platform with RAG}. The
project contributed to SCIENTIAE's development of a sovereign and
domain-adaptable Graph-RAG framework by implementing and evaluating an
instance dedicated to political and geopolitical analysis. Its purpose was to
transform heterogeneous public information into a structured, searchable,
traceable, and explainable knowledge layer capable of supporting factual,
relational, temporal, and comparative questions.

The resulting platform integrates autonomous acquisition through RSS
monitoring, query-driven discovery, direct URL ingestion, and PDF parsing.
Documents are normalized and preserved with their provenance before
LLM-based entity and relation extraction. DSPy is used at this ingestion stage
to improve the reliability, structural validity, and efficiency of knowledge
extraction. Extracted knowledge is published in Neo4j, textual evidence is
indexed in ZVec, and source documents are retained in MongoDB. These layers
support a Graph-RAG reasoning engine that combines semantic retrieval, graph
traversal, cited answer generation, and three selectable reasoning modes. The
analytical interface connects these capabilities to conversational querying,
document consultation, graph exploration, specialized views, and
administrative supervision.

The system-level evaluation used a curated benchmark of \textbf{150
questions}, producing \textbf{450 executions} across standard RAG,
chain-of-thought, and reflexive reasoning. RAGAS-based results showed a
progressive improvement with reasoning depth: average answer correctness rose
from \textbf{0.52} for standard RAG to \textbf{0.65} for chain-of-thought and
\textbf{0.80} for reflexive reasoning. The reflexive mode also achieved the
strongest overall relevancy, faithfulness, and context-recall profile, although
its additional retrieval and verification steps increased latency.

The embedding comparison demonstrated that the Mistral embedding
implementation reduced average response time from \textbf{15 to 5 seconds}
for standard RAG, from \textbf{30 to 15 seconds} for chain-of-thought, and
from \textbf{90 to 50 seconds} for reflexive reasoning, while improving the
reported retrieval-quality indicators. At the ingestion stage, DSPy
optimization increased the composite extraction score from \textbf{0.723} to
\textbf{0.883} and reduced mean processing latency from \textbf{4.62} to
\textbf{3.21 seconds}. Together, these findings show that system quality
depends on both downstream reasoning and upstream evidence construction.

The principal technical challenges concerned reliable corpus construction,
entity consistency, retrieval completeness, and operational traceability.
Heterogeneous and continuously evolving sources required deduplication,
metadata preservation, scheduling, and administrative visibility. Noisy
articles and ambiguous names affected graph quality, while missing evidence
and chunk boundaries limited some answers independently of the reasoning
model. These constraints motivated deterministic entity resolution,
DSPy-optimized extraction, hybrid vector-and-graph retrieval, reflexive
verification, and end-to-end observability.

From a professional perspective, this project provided valuable experience in the end-to-end construction of an AI-assisted analytical platform. It required work across data acquisition, backend services, NLP pipelines, vector indexing, graph databases, frontend interfaces, containerized deployment, observability, and evaluation. The internship therefore strengthened both technical skills and engineering discipline, especially in debugging distributed components, validating generated answers, and designing systems where traceability is essential.

Although the developed platform reaches a functional and evaluable state, several perspectives remain open. The first direction concerns retrieval improvement. Future work should investigate graph-aware reranking, stronger query expansion, temporal filtering, and more systematic chunking strategies in order to increase context recall and precision. Re-embedding the corpus with higher-dimensional multilingual models would also be relevant, especially as the platform evolves toward French and Arabic political content.

A second perspective concerns the enrichment of the knowledge graph. Future versions could introduce stronger temporal validity intervals on relations, better event normalization, confidence scores for extracted facts, and more advanced entity disambiguation. These improvements would make the graph more reliable for longitudinal political analysis and for questions involving institutional evolution, alliances, conflicts, or changes in position over time.

A third perspective concerns evaluation. Although the 150-question benchmark
supports comparison across question categories and reasoning modes, it cannot
represent every geopolitical region, language, event type, or analytical
formulation. Future evaluation should incorporate human analyst ratings,
additional multilingual and temporal questions, confidence calibration, and
adversarial cases requiring abstention or careful qualification.

Finally, the platform could be strengthened operationally through continuous ingestion monitoring, richer administrator dashboards, improved observability, and deployment hardening. These improvements would help transform the prototype into a more robust decision-support tool that can be maintained, audited, and extended in a production environment.

In conclusion, this project demonstrates the feasibility of a sovereign and
inspectable Graph-RAG framework whose core acquisition, extraction,
structuring, retrieval, reasoning, and evaluation components can be adapted
to different domains. Its political and geopolitical implementation shows
that unstructured information can be transformed into connected evidence and
grounded answers with explicit sources. It also confirms that dependable
Graph-RAG behavior is a system-level property: it depends not only on the
language model, but also on corpus quality, extraction reliability, knowledge
representation, retrieval strategy, observability, and evaluation design.
